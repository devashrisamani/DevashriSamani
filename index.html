Ancestral Echoes

South Australia during World War 1

Project Overview

Ancestral Echoes is an interactive website with a central puzzle game designed to entertain and educate users about South Australia's role in World War I. It targets young individuals aged 10 to 16, especially from South Australia and uses various data sources to create an engaging historical experience. This website offers an enjoyable way to learn about local history through an immersive game.

Observations from the Australian local history education research: Upon reviewing the Australian curriculum, specifically the content descriptions and achievement standards for students in years 9 and 10 , it's clear that they should be exposed to World War I events, the Australian wartime experience, and the importance of the Anzac legend. However, historical knowledge can be dense, and traditional classroom teaching methods can be uninteresting and overwhelming for high school students, inundating them with excessive information. A history education study conducted by Western Sydney University involved surveying 700 Australian students aged 12 to 18 (Hyland, 2023). The results showed that about two-thirds of these students did not feel a connection to certain historical events, such as those related to the Anzacs or the Federation (Hyland, 2023). Many students perceived Anzac history as irrelevant to their lives, despite having basic knowledge of World War I acquired through school. The lack of engagement and interest stemmed from the perception that these historical events occurred a long time ago and seemed distant from their contemporary lives (Hyland, 2023). Notably, some research has indicated an ongoing improvement in teaching methods. Educators are increasingly integrating multimedia resources, such as newspapers, radio broadcasts, and photographs, in an effort to present content in a more appealing manner to students (Hyland, 2023).

Observations from Gamification in historical education: According to the research about gamification in historical education, 97.5% of students stated that gamification learning activities could spark excitement. (Moseikina, M, 2022). There is a significant increase in their learning motivation when using gamified learning methods compared to traditional education. This approach maintained their interest in the subject, encouraged active learning through social interactions (Moseikina, M, 2022). 95% of students expressed satisfaction with the learning process, finding it easy, engaging, and valuable (Moseikina, M, 2022). The pedagogical model of gamified history education, as tested by the students, has proven to be highly effective in imparting fundamental historical knowledge and fostering a professional level of historical thinking (Moseikina, M, 2022). Therefore, based on the above research, our website is designed to encourage our audience to view history from a more specific and individualized perspective. They could sense the lives of South Australians on both the front lines and the home front during WWI, tangibly feeling the impact of the war on ordinary people, and recognizing the value of peace.

Initial Concept: Our initial concept is about creating a puzzle game that enables users to uncover Queensland's history by visualizing news article images and local photo stories from the past through puzzles. After users solve the puzzles, they will unlock key highlights related to the images and articles. The primary internal data source we used is from the State Library of Queensland named “The Queenslander newspaper photographs 1914-1918”. While searching for the external datasets, we were aiming to seek datasets including information on world history during 1914-1918 like WW1. Therefore, it can be combined with our internal data to help draw connections between Queensland and global historical events.

Purpose: The primary purpose of the website is to provide a platform for young individuals to interact with their local history. Our website emerged from the observation that many existing history-related platforms, like Britannica's Today in History or applications similar to History at a Glance, often present historical information in a conventional and overwhelming manner. These platforms primarily rely on news articles, images, and data but tend to lack engaging interactions. Additionally, our research uncovered a gap in platforms providing South Australian news articles related to World War I. This gap results in young individuals, particularly those in South Australia, having limited knowledge about their region's significant contributions to World War I. Furthermore, interviews with students living in South Australia highlighted their lack of awareness regarding the region's local history and its contributions to World War I. Ancestral Echoes aims to tackle these challenges by making history more interactive, particularly for the younger generation. We understand that young individuals seek entertainment through various forms, such as board games and music, and are actively engaged on social media. Traditional history textbooks often present abstract and grand narratives that feel disconnected from their daily lives, making it difficult to establish a connection.

User Persona: Our website is designed for individuals like Alice, young and curious, interested in local history but struggling to find engaging ways to explore it. Traditional methods, such as library visits, feel outdated, and knowledge from older generations is limited. History textbooks often seem abstract and distant. Our target audience primarily consists of young individuals, like Alice, who have a basic understanding of World War I history but want a more immersive and entertaining way to learn. Our research highlights the need for interactive platforms that delve into the specific details of South Australia's contributions to World War I. We also discovered through interviews with students in South Australia that there is a lack of awareness about local history and its role in World War I among this group. The research findings lead us to the conclusion that there is a lack of interactive platforms that offer engaging methods for learning about the specific details of South Australia's contributions to World War I. Additionally, we conducted interviews with students residing in South Australia . Most of them are unaware regarding the region's local history and its role in World War I. The following research observations helped us define the target audience and user persona.

User Story: Having understood the needs of our target audience, as previously explained, we have envisioned a user scenario as follows: In order to complete the assignment involves researching Australia's contributions to WW1. Alice discovers our website "Ancestral Echoes”. After engaging with a puzzle game featuring a historical photograph, she learnt the story behind the image. This particular image captures the Voluntary Nursing Corps in Balaklava, South Australia, which happens to be her grandmother's hometown. This discovery forms a connection between Alice and the history story. Eager to delve deeper into her newfound knowledge, Alice follows a link to Wikipedia, further enriching her understanding. After all, she shares this experience on Instagram, and her peers respond with enthusiasm.

2D: MVP Presentation

Partial Implementation
Development Process

The development process includes building the puzzle canvas and using JavaScript to define the basic fucntions of the game. Above mentioned is the code snippet that demonstrates how the game is initialised.

My contribution towards Coding and Web Development

I used a variety of programmatic components which were strategically designed and implemented to provide a rich and engaging user experience for the puzzle game. Below, we elaborate on the key functions that played integral role in our project:

ImageBlock Constructor: I introduced the ImageBlock constructor function to serve as the blueprint for individual jigsaw puzzle pieces. It endowed each piece with essential properties, including a unique piece number, coordinates (both X and Y), and a selection status flag. These properties formed the foundation for the interactive manipulation of puzzle pieces.

Jigsaw Constructor: At the core of our project, the Jigsaw constructor function took center stage. This function bore the responsibility of initializing the entire game. It not only configured the canvas for rendering but also orchestrated the placement of puzzle pieces and user interactions. By encapsulating these vital aspects within the Jigsaw constructor, we realized a modular and maintainable codebase.

Initialization Function: We introduced the initDrawing function to kickstart the jigsaw puzzle game. Its role was pivotal in setting up event listeners to capture user interactions, such as mouse clicks and movements. Subsequently, this function triggered the commencement of a new game session.

Preview Display Function: To provide users with a visual reference, the showPreview function was incorporated. It displayed a preview of the final puzzle image, offering users a sneak peek at the ultimate objective of the game.

Game Initialization Function: When initiating a fresh game, the initializeNewGame function came into action. This function orchestrated the arrangement of image blocks (representing puzzle pieces) and the rendering of the game grid on the canvas, thereby presenting users with a new puzzle challenge.

Position Setting Function: The setImageBlock function was essential for accurately positioning individual image blocks within the puzzle grid. It contributed significantly to the creation of a coherent puzzle layout, facilitating user engagement.

Grid Line Drawing Function: The drawLines function was responsible for visually demarcating the puzzle grid by drawing grid lines on the canvas. This feature aided users in correctly positioning the puzzle pieces.

Image Rendering Functions: Various functions were dedicated to the rendering of puzzle pieces. drawAllImages efficiently displayed all image blocks on the canvas, excluding the currently selected one. drawImageBlock focused on rendering an individual image block, particularly during user interactions. The drawFinalImage function was triggered upon puzzle completion, providing users with a clear view of the final image.

Completion Handling Function: The onFinished function was instrumental in executing actions when the puzzle was successfully completed. These actions included audio playback, board clearing, and the presentation of an information page to celebrate the user's achievement.

Board Clearing Function: When the puzzle was completed, the clearGame function efficiently cleared the game board by removing the pieces, thus preparing the canvas for a new game session.

Mouse Event Handlers: Functions such as handleOnMouseOut, handleOnMouseDown, handleOnMouseUp, and handleOnMouseMove catered to the management of user interactions. They enabled the selection, dropping, and movement of puzzle pieces, fostering a dynamic gameplay experience.

Canvas Clearing Function: To facilitate game resets and maintain a clean canvas, I introduced the clear function, which effectively wiped the canvas clean.

Random Number Generator Function: Serving as a random number generator within specified minimum and maximum value ranges. This function introduced an element of randomness into the gameplay.

Image Block Identification Functions: For precise tracking of puzzle pieces, I implemented the getImageBlock function, allowing identification based on user mouse coordinates. Additionally, the getImageBlockOnEqual function helped identify specific puzzle pieces with matching coordinates.

Completion Checker Function: The isFinished function played a pivotal role in determining whether the puzzle was successfully completed. By comparing the positions of image blocks to their target positions, it ensured that all pieces were correctly arranged.

Course Outcomes
I found this course to be quite enjoyable. The freedom to unleash our creativity was a highlight for me, and I was intrigued by the diverse directions that everyone took with their projects. However, my primary issue with the course is the extensive documentation requirements. I felt that we spent a considerable amount of time on report writing, which limited the time available for designing and creating our final product.
Course Improvements
Reflecting on my initial expectations and experiences throughout the course, there are several key observations and recommendations to consider. The course didn't progress well for students with prior website development experience who wanted to go beyond the basic requirements. The resistance to incorporating certain technologies limited the creative potential of some projects. It is suggested that the course should be more open to embracing innovative technologies and approaches, provided they align with the learning objectives. The course's emphasis on large-scale assessment prompted an approach within teams, where each team member experienced only a fraction of the design process. This limited the depth of learning and exploration. A recommendation is to introduce activities where each team member must present their research to the team, promoting a more comprehensive understanding of the project as a whole. Another observation was the reluctance of many teams to initiate user-testing and actively seek feedback. Teams often relied on tutor-directed activities for feedback, and peer interactions were limited. Encouraging the habit of seeking feedback independently would be beneficial. In our experience, obtaining feedback from potential users significantly contributed to our project's success by helping us identify and rectify issues promptly. Regarding team management, my team faced challenges with a team member who did not contribute equally to the project. The course did little to assist in addressing this issue, and the responsibility to address the problem ultimately fell on the team. It is recommended that the course implement regular peer performance evaluations to identify and address such issues earlier in the process. Placing the onus on teams to deal with poor performance in a first-year course may not be the most effective approach.
